What Are Reasearchers Doing About Insomnia?

By Adi

Insomnia is a common sleep disorder that affects millions of people worldwide. It is characterized by dissatisfaction with falling asleep, staying asleep, or both. Insomnia can be acute (short-term) or chronic (long-term). There isn’t a universally accepted model for this disorder due to there being a large variety of causes. Nevertheless, researchers are working on a model that can account for the diversity of symptoms and causes and find the best treatment.

Do you think you suffer from insomnia?

According to Levenson, Kay, & Buysse (2015), prevalence rates of insomnia disorder are between 5% to 15% of people worldwide. It is the second-most common mental disorder. Doctors typically diagnosis this by asking, “Do you experience difficulty sleeping?” or “Do you have difficulty staying asleep?” These questions on insomnia are a way to clue into what underlying cause may be at play. Other objective measures like actigraphy or polysomnography are not (yet) part of most routine diagnoses. Here is the DSM-5-TR checklist by Psych Central diagnoses.

Hyperarousal

Hyperarousal tends to be a common overarching symptom. It is theorized that a state of hypervigilance during the day can lead to difficulty initiating or maintaining sleep at night. This state is measured by “cortisol, heart rate variability, EEG, or even self-report.” (Levenson, Kay, & Buysse, 2015) A common scenario is: “Once an individual begins to experience sleep difficulties, worry and rumination shift from life events to worries about sleep itself and about the daytime consequences of not getting enough sleep. This negatively-toned cognitive activity is further fueled if a sleep-related threat is detected or a sleep deficit is perceived.” (Roth T., 2007) A way to prevent this is to calm your mind and do less mind-intensive tasks like folding clothes before sleep. If you realize you are not falling asleep, get up for 10-15 minutes, do some stretches, and get your mind off the stressor.

Could it be genetic?

Early studies were done on flies to find potential genes responsible for insomnia. It is estimated, based on human studies, that the disorder is 31% to 58% heritable (Levenson, Kay, & Buysse, 2015). If someone in your family has insomnia, you may also be predisposed to having the disorder. “Predisposing” factors come from the areas of (epi-)genetics and early life stress that contribute to individual differences at the level of brain function and personality. (Riemann et al., 2022)



What molecules are involved

While the studies go into more nuances, we can group these molecules as ones that make you sleepy and ones that keep you awake. Keep your sleep: γ-aminobutyric acid [GABA], adenosine, serotonin, melatonin, prostaglandin D2 (Levenson, Kay, & Buysse, 2015) Keep you awake: catecholamines, orexin, and histamine It isn’t as simple as taking pills for sleepiness hormones, as the body needs to create a balance to function. This depiction of hormones is also a vast oversimplification. Some studies show some promise with taking melatonin. However, there are some side effects, and more research needs to be conducted to see if this has uses beyond those with serious medical complications. (Besag, 2019)
